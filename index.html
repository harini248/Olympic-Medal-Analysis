Overall Introduction

Why Olympic Medal Distribution Matters

The Olympic Games represent the largest international sporting competition in the world, bringing together athletes from nearly every nation across hundreds of events. Since the modern Games began in Athens in 1896, the Olympics have grown dramatically in scale and global participation. Yet beneath the spectacle of athletic achievement, the distribution of medals tells a more complicated story about power, resources, and inequality on a global scale.

Understanding how Olympic medals are distributed across countries and over time reveals patterns that go far beyond sport. Nations with stronger economies, larger populations, and greater government investment in athletic development tend to win disproportionately more medals. This creates a persistent concentration of success among a small group of dominant countries, raising important questions about what medal counts actually measure and whether they reflect true athletic achievement or simply reflect structural advantages.

"Olympic medal counts are not just a record of athletic achievement. They are a reflection of a nation's resources, history, and place in the global order."

This project analyzes 120 years of Olympic data to explore how medal distribution has shifted over time, which nations have dominated, and what factors may contribute to those patterns. We also examine how participation has changed across gender and season, offering a broader picture of how the Olympics have evolved as a global institution.

Introduction to the Data

120 Years of Athletes & Results

Our analysis uses the Kaggle dataset "120 years of Olympic history: athletes and results," compiled by rgriffin and originally scraped from sports-reference.com in May 2018. The dataset covers the modern Olympic Games from Athens 1896 through Rio 2016 and contains 271,116 rows, where each row represents one athlete competing in one event. With 15 features spanning athlete demographics, competition identifiers, and outcome information, it provides a rich foundation for exploring long-term trends in medal distribution.

For our analysis, we filtered the data to include only medal-winning athletes and deduplicated records by Year, Season, Event, NOC, and Medal to avoid overcounting team events. This ensures that a single gold medal in a team relay is counted once per country rather than once per athlete. The dataset does not include external variables such as GDP or population, so our analysis focuses on describing and comparing medal patterns rather than explaining their causes.

Overview of Medal Distribution

Medal Counts Over Time

This visualization shows how Olympic medal counts have changed over time, separated by medal type (Gold, Silver, Bronze) and by Olympic season (Summer vs. Winter). Medal-winning records were deduplicated by Year, Season, Event, NOC, and Medal to avoid overcounting team events. Summer and Winter Olympics are shown separately to avoid misleading comparisons, as they follow different schedules and scales.

From the chart, we see a clear long-term increase in medal counts, especially in the Summer Olympics, reflecting the expansion of events and global participation over time. There are also noticeable gaps during periods like World War I and World War II, where the Games were not held. Overall, the visualization highlights both the growth of the Olympics and the structural differences between Summer and Winter competitions.

Medal Distribution by Country

Who Wins, and How Much?

This interactive stacked bar chart compares medal distributions among the top 10 countries, with a dropdown to toggle between Summer and Winter Games. Use the dropdown to switch seasons and hover over any bar to see the exact Gold, Silver, and Bronze breakdown for that country.

The stacked bar chart shows how each country's medals are divided across Gold, Silver, and Bronze, while also highlighting differences in overall medal volume. A key takeaway is that Olympic success is concentrated among a small group of dominant nations, but the leading countries vary between Summer and Winter Olympics due to differences in sports, geography, and competitive specialization. The interactive filter makes it easier to directly compare these patterns across seasons.

Geographic Distribution of Medals

Mapping Olympic Success Around the World

This choropleth map shows the geographic distribution of Olympic medals across countries in the Summer Games. Records were deduplicated and filtered to Summer Olympics only, then aggregated by country using each nation's three-letter NOC code. A logarithmic color scale is used because a small number of countries account for a disproportionately large share of total medals, which would make most of the world appear identical on a linear scale.

The resulting pattern highlights a strong concentration of Olympic success in North America, Europe, and parts of Asia, while many other countries have much lower totals. Overall, this visualization reinforces the idea that Olympic medal distribution is highly uneven across the world.

Gender Representation Over Time

Progress Toward Gender Parity

This area chart shows the gender breakdown of medal-winning athletes over time, normalized to proportions so each year sums to 100%. Use the dropdown to switch between Summer and Winter Olympics.

The chart reveals that early Olympic Games were almost entirely dominated by male athletes, with women representing only a very small share. Over time, however, the proportion of female medal-winning athletes increases steadily, especially in the Summer Olympics, approaching near parity in recent decades. This highlights the significant progress toward gender balance in Olympic competition.

Country Medal Trajectory

One Country, 120 Years

This interactive visualization allows you to select any country using the dropdown and explore its medal trajectory across every Olympic year. Unlike the country comparison chart which shows the top 10 nations side by side, this chart focuses on a single country's full history, broken down by Gold, Silver, and Bronze. The data was deduplicated by Year, Season, Event, NOC, and Medal to ensure team events are not overcounted. Use the dropdown to select a country by its three-letter NOC code and hover over any point for exact medal counts by year.

Selecting the United States (USA) reveals a dominant and consistent trajectory across the entire 120-year span, with notable dips during boycott years like 1980. Selecting China (CHN) illustrates how a nation can go from near-zero medals to one of the top performers in just a few decades. The visualization makes it easy to compare how different countries have risen, fallen, or stayed consistent over Olympic history.

Summary & Additional Work

Conclusions and Future Directions

This project explored 120 years of Olympic medal distribution through five visualizations covering trends over time, country-level comparisons, geographic patterns, gender representation, and individual country trajectories. Taken together, these visualizations paint a consistent picture: Olympic success has historically been concentrated among a small number of wealthy, resource-rich nations, and the structure of that inequality has remained largely stable even as the Games have expanded in scale and global participation.

At the same time, the data reveals meaningful progress in at least one dimension. Gender representation among medal-winning athletes has shifted dramatically since the early 1900s, with women now approaching near parity in the Summer Olympics. This reflects decades of policy changes, advocacy, and institutional reform within the Olympic movement.

Key Findings

Medal counts have grown steadily since 1896, driven by the expansion of events and participating nations. The United States, Soviet Union, and a small group of European nations account for a disproportionate share of all medals ever awarded. Geographic concentration is strongest in North America, Western Europe, and parts of Asia.

Limitations

The dataset ends at Rio 2016 and does not include Tokyo 2020 or Paris 2024. It also lacks external variables like GDP, population, or government sports funding, which would be necessary to explain why certain nations dominate. Medal counts also do not account for the size of a country's delegation or the number of events it enters.

Future Work

Future analysis could incorporate GDP per capita and population data to compute medals-per-capita rankings, offering a fairer cross-national comparison. Adding data from more recent Games would also allow for analysis of emerging athletic powers. A time-series breakdown by sport category could reveal which disciplines drive national medal totals.
